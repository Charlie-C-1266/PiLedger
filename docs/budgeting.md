# Budgeting with Envelopes

PiLedger's Budget screen is built around **zero-based, envelope-style budgeting** — the same idea behind the old "cash in labelled envelopes" method, done digitally. If you're new to it, this guide explains the idea and walks you through setting up your first budget.

## The core idea: give every pound a job

A zero-based budget starts from a simple rule:

> **Income − everything you assign = 0**

You don't budget what you *think* you'll spend and hope it works out. Instead you take the money you actually expect to receive and hand all of it out — to bills, to groceries, to savings, to fun — until there's nothing left *unassigned*. Every pound has a job before the month begins.

This is what the **envelopes** are. Each envelope is a labelled pot ("Groceries", "Rent", "Eating out") that you fill with a planned amount. As you spend, the envelope drains. When it's empty, that's your signal to stop — or to deliberately move money from another envelope to cover it.

The pay-off is awareness: instead of one big balance you're afraid to look at, you have a handful of small, named limits that tell you exactly where you stand.

## How it maps to PiLedger

The Budget screen has four building blocks. Here's how they fit together:

| Concept | What it is | Where it lives |
|---|---|---|
| **Income** | The money you expect each month. Add as many sources as you like (salary, side income, etc.). | The income card, top-left. |
| **Group** | A heading that organises related envelopes — e.g. *Bills & Housing*, *Everyday*, *Savings*. Each group has a colour. | The "+ Add group" button. |
| **Envelope** | A single spending pot inside a group, pinned to one **category**. | "+ Add envelope" inside a group. |
| **Allocation** | The total you've assigned across every envelope. Your goal is for this to equal your income. | The hero bar, top of the page. |

The **hero** at the top shows income versus allocation at a glance. When the two match — everything assigned, nothing left over — your zero-based budget is balanced.

### Envelopes track real spending automatically

Each envelope is tied to a **category** (a built-in one like *Groceries*, or one of your own). You don't tick off spending by hand: PiLedger looks at your transactions in that category for the current month, converts them to your base currency, and fills in the envelope's **spent** figure for you. Budget on the Budget screen, spend as normal on your accounts, and the two line up on their own.

### Flexible vs fixed groups

When you create a group you choose **Flexible** or **Fixed**:

- **Fixed** — bills that don't move: rent, subscriptions, loan payments. You can't really "spend less" on them this month, so they sit outside your day-to-day spending room.
- **Flexible** — everyday, discretionary spending: groceries, eating out, hobbies. These are the envelopes you actually steer week to week.

Only **flexible** groups feed your **Safe to spend** figure (below), so the distinction keeps that number honest — it reflects the money you can genuinely flex, not your rent.

## Setting up your first budget

1. **Add your income.** Start with what you actually expect to receive, not a round number. If pay varies, use a conservative estimate — you can always assign a surplus later.
2. **Create a few groups.** Three or four is plenty to start: something like *Bills*, *Everyday*, and *Savings*. Mark the bills group **Fixed** and the rest **Flexible**.
3. **Add envelopes to each group** and pin each to a category. Begin with your biggest, most predictable costs (rent, groceries) — they're the easiest to estimate.
4. **Assign every pound.** Keep adding to envelopes until the hero shows nothing left to budget. If you run out before everything's covered, you've found a real constraint — trim an envelope rather than ignoring it. If you have money spare, give it a job: more into savings is a perfectly good answer.
5. **Spend as normal.** Record transactions against your accounts the way you always do. Envelopes fill in their **spent** automatically as the month goes on.

> **Tip:** Don't aim for a perfect budget on day one. The first month is a rough draft — you'll discover an envelope is too tight or too generous, adjust it, and the next month is closer. That's the method working, not failing.

## Reading the right-hand rail

Once your budget has data, the cards on the right tell you how it's going:

- **Safe to spend** — what's left across your *flexible* envelopes for the period. This is the one number to watch day to day: it's the cushion you can dip into without breaking the plan.
- **Allocation** (the donut) — how your assigned money splits across groups, by colour. A quick gut-check that the shares match your priorities.
- **Spent so far** — total spent against total allocated this month, plus how many envelopes you're running.
- **Budget vs actual** — a trend of what you budgeted against what you actually spent over recent months, so you can see which envelopes consistently run hot or cold and tune them.

The **period toggle** (Monthly / Weekly / Yearly) only rescales the figures on screen — weekly and yearly are just your monthly numbers divided or multiplied for a different view. It never changes the underlying budget.

## Tips for making it stick

- **Start rough, refine monthly.** Your categories and amounts will drift toward reality after a couple of cycles. Expect it.
- **Keep an eye on Safe to spend, not your account balance.** The account balance includes money already promised to bills; Safe to spend is what's genuinely free.
- **When an envelope runs out, move money — don't ignore it.** Covering an overspend by trimming another envelope keeps the budget zero-based and honest. Pretending it didn't happen doesn't.
- **Give savings an envelope too.** Treating savings as a planned "expense" you assign up front is far more reliable than saving whatever happens to be left at month-end.
- **Fewer, broader envelopes beat many tiny ones** when you're starting out. You can always split *Everyday* into *Groceries* and *Eating out* once you want the detail.
